# Roadmap

Where Oubliette is headed. This is a direction, not a promise — priorities shift with what players actually ask for. Have an opinion? Open an issue.

## Now — v1.0

The current release. The full game, the Forge, and the Arena are all here and playable; see [FEATURES.md](FEATURES.md) for everything that already works, and [Known issues](README.md#known-issues) for the rough edges.

**New in v1.0:** **voiced narration** (a local narrator reads the DM aloud — no cloud, no keys), **persistent companions** (the wolf pup you bought stays, grows, and fights at your side with its real kit), the **living world** (keyed encounters, factions with standing, a campaign clock, and scheduled world events — the world moves whether you attend or not), **trinkets** for the journal, and a **racial-traits pass** (Relentless Endurance actually catches the half-orc now, dragonborn choose a draconic ancestry and get their breath weapon, dwarves resist poison, and more). All described below.

**Fresh on main since v1.0:** a **session cost meter** for Anthropic tables (exact token counts and a dollar estimate in the ☰ menu, with optional enter-your-own prices so any model reads true), **prompt caching** (the DM's unchanging rulebook, tools, and campaign memory bill at cache rates while you play — a long campaign's growing memory no longer grows the per-turn cost), and Forge coin that names itself (authored money accepts "5 sp" or "1 gp 5 sp" anywhere a bare number used to mean gold).

## Later — candidates for v2.0

Unordered, and gated on demand. The big themes:

### Deeper world-building in the Forge
- **Reskins** — rename and reflavor an SRD class or race ("Tide Warden" that plays as a ranger) with no mechanical work.
- **Per-world SRD allow/deny lists** — a world can say "no warlocks, no dragonborn" and character creation honors it.
- **Custom subclasses** and structured custom races — more of your own mechanics, not just prose.
- **Form-based editing for advanced monster traits** — legendary resistance, regeneration, lair actions, and monster spellcasting are all supported by the engine today, but currently need the raw-data editor.
- **A manifest editor** — edit a world's name, author, and description directly in the Forge.

### Richer worlds
- **Keyed encounters** *(shipped in v1.0)* — the fights the author promised, kept: bind monsters to a place with trigger conditions (first visit, any visit, by night, at a party level — or only when a world event arms them) and they *happen*, every campaign. The AI DM narrates the approach; the engine stages the fight — authored encounters skip the improvisation budget, because the author knows best. Authored in a place's new "Keyed encounters" drawer in the Forge.
- **Factions** *(shipped in v1.0)* — first-class powers with membership, standing, and secret agendas. The party holds a code-owned standing score with each faction, spoken everywhere in five tiers (hostile → allied): quests carry the big authored shifts (with per-outcome filters — sparing the smuggler pleases one power and angers another), the AI DM nudges within a hard cap when the fiction earns it, standing can gate quests like a level requirement, and NPCs wear their allegiance so the DM plays them loyal. Players get a Factions page — unmet powers show as **???** until the world reveals them.
- **Timed and scheduled world events** *(shipped in v1.0)* — the world moves whether you attend or not. Authors schedule happenings against a real campaign clock (a new day counter driven by nights slept and roads travelled, with two-pin travel-time calibration on the world map): fire on a day, repeat on a rhythm, or wait for a quest or a faction's trust. An event can shift standings in the dark, open or close quest offers, turn the weather, or arm a keyed encounter — and if the party is elsewhere when it happens, the news reaches them as rumor.
- **Persistent NPC companions** *(shipped in v1.0)* — the mercenary you talked out of a bad contract, the wolf pup you bought at the kennel: they stop evaporating when the scene ends. The DM proposes, **you** confirm, and from then on they travel on the party card, fight at your side in every Arena battle (player-controlled — no AI pet walking into lava), and count toward how dangerous the world is allowed to be. A creature fights with its real monster kit, too — your wolf gets its knock-them-prone bite and pack tactics, not a generic swing. People level with real classes at the party's pace; creatures grow only if their world's author wrote growth stages for them ("pseudodragon → adolescent → drake", authored in the Forge) — the corner-shop wolf stays a wolf, loyally. Growth lands as a narrated story moment, not a spreadsheet event. On tables with companion death switched on, losing them is permanent — and means something. Party cap: 6, counting everyone.

### Rules & engine
- **Difficulty & challenge settings** *(shipped in v0.9.2)* — decide how dangerous your world feels. Choose a difficulty when you start a campaign (Story, Adventure, Challenge, or Hardcore, or set the dials yourself), changeable later in Settings. It caps the challenge rating of the encounters the AI improvises to the party's real strength, gates long rests behind the fiction (a night needs a safe-enough moment and costs lodging coin or rations, with interrupted camps on the harshest settings), lets Forge quests declare a minimum party level ("starts at party level 3+") that stays hidden until you've earned it, and gives the DM a clear read on the party's strength when it sizes up a fight. Pairs naturally with keyed encounters to shape a campaign's arc. Home, too, to hardcore mode: if the whole party falls, the DM narrates the end of the story, writes the campaign's final chapter, and says goodbye — that save is over for real.
- **House rules per world** *(built — in testing on main)* — a world's author picks its table variants in the Forge, the engine enforces them, and players see them read-only in Settings (the author decides, so no toggle can break the game). The first menu: **side initiative** (all heroes act, then all foes) or **re-roll initiative** every round; **flanking** (melee advantage when allies sandwich a foe); **crits on 19–20**; **brutal crits** (critical dice come in maximized); and **potions as a bonus action**. Everything off = play exactly by the book. Custom *conditions* aren't planned — the SRD's own condition set covers what 5e itself does, and reskinning an existing condition in the Forge is the supported path.
- **Attunement enforcement** *(built — in testing on main)* — magic items marked "requires attunement" are now truly inert until a hero bonds with them, and each hero holds at most three bonds. Attuning (or letting a bond go) happens when the party rests — the rest popup offers each hero's choices — and a bond breaks on its own if the item is handed away or sold. New characters start already attuned to their starting relics, and the DM knows a dormant ring from a working one.
- **Arena test beds** - add the ability to preview spells, custom attack animations, or run simulations using existing combat AI to balance custom monsters and classes.
- **The last SRD combat gaps** *(built — in testing on main)* — **Surprise and ambushes**: a side genuinely caught off guard loses its opening turn and can't react until its first turn passes. The AI DM judges surprise from the fiction (both directions — spring a trap or walk into one), world authors can mark a keyed encounter as a true ambush in the Forge, and the Alert feat now actually works (its +5 initiative and can't-be-surprised both reach combat). **Destructible walls**: attack a wall and break through it — conjured walls use their spell's panel HP (Wall of Force remains untouchable, as written), and a battlefield's author can give its walls hit points in the Forge (empty = permanent scenery). A broken wall opens movement and sightlines immediately. **Sanctuary** turned out to already be enforced (shipped quietly with the spell wave) — the ward's save DC, ward-breaks-if-you-attack, and the lost swing on a failed Wisdom save were all live.

### The experience
- **Voiced narration** *(shipped in v1.0)* — one local narrator voice reads the DM's turns aloud, entirely on your machine: no cloud, no keys, works in Offline Mode. Two voice tiers, chosen at setup with a sample clip you can hear first: **Kokoro 82M** (instant and light, runs on any CPU) and **Qwen3-TTS 1.7B** (the expressive one — nine speakers with an audiobook-narrator direction; needs a GPU with ~2 GB of memory to spare and a ~2 GB download). Read-along mode streams the text ahead of the voice; movie mode reveals it in step with the reading. Enabling narration runs a self-benchmark on your actual hardware and says honestly if the chosen tier is too slow. The DM prompt is never touched — narration off is exactly today's game.
- **Trinkets** *(shipped in v1.0)* — optional authored quest add-ons that can be stored in the player journal. A fragment of a map, a note from a powerful noble, etc.
- **Upgraded player journal** *(shipped in v0.9.2)* — the journal is now a real book: light parchment pages in a leather binding, opened from its cover. Pick your character's handwriting (six bundled hands), ink color, cover leather, cover emblem, and paper style at the Bookbinder; write directly on the page with bold/italics/underline/strikethrough, three text sizes, colored inks, and a pack of highlighters; stamp entries with wax seals ("In progress", "Done", or your own words). Entries flow across numbered pages with a page-turn, and a new journal opens with Quests/People/Places/Creatures tabs waiting for ink. Old notes carry over untouched. The page receives trinkets (see above) as of v1.0.
