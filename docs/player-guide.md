# Playing Oubliette Table — a guide to talking with your DM

Oubliette's DM is a live AI (we build and test against **Claude Sonnet 5** —
other models will behave differently, and you're on your own tuning for them).
It's a genuinely good DM: it remembers, it plans, it plays fair. But like any
DM, the game it runs is only as good as what you give it. This guide comes from
a real observed playtest — every rule below earned its place at the table.

## The golden rule: volley the ball back

The DM builds its scenes almost entirely out of what *you* write. A bare
"i attack" or "go north" starves it. You don't need to write a novel — one
solid, punctuated paragraph per turn is the sweet spot:

- **What you do** (the action),
- **why or how** (the intent and flavor),
- **what you say, in "quotation marks"** (actual dialogue).

> *I catch the eye of a friendly-looking dockhand coiling rope nearby and ask,
> "If a man wanted to sing for his supper in Brightvale, where would he go?"*

That one message got back a scene, a named venue, and a plot hook. The same
turn written as "ask dockhand about work" gets back a shrug.

## One scene-beat per message

The system resolves **one** action (and rolls at most one check) per turn.
If you pack travel + a conversation + a plan for tomorrow into one message,
something gets shortchanged. Especially: **never straddle sleep in one
message** ("I go to bed, then in the morning I…"). The night ends the scene;
give the morning its own turn.

## Quotes are speech. Everything else is visible.

The DM treats your message as what your character observably does. Unquoted
musings can leak — an NPC may react to something you meant as a private
thought. If it must stay private, keep it out of the message (or mark it
plainly: *privately, to myself, I wonder…*).

## When the world stonewalls you, it's the dice

Every meaningful attempt gets a real, hidden-in-plain-sight roll against your
actual character sheet. Failures aren't error messages — they're narrated as
fiction: the barkeep's face closes, the crowd drifts, the sea "resists being
read." Our playtest bard once failed six social and perception checks in a row
and the world simply, believably, kept its secrets.

So when you're stonewalled:

- **Don't re-ask louder.** The roll happened; repeating the same approach just
  rolls the same odds against a wearier audience.
- **Change the approach or the audience.** The fishers wouldn't talk at night
  by the fire; the net-menders talked in the morning with busy hands.
- **Play to your sheet.** A silver-tongued bard should talk, not skulk. The
  checks use your real modifiers.
- **Borrow competence.** NPCs are good at things you aren't. Our bard failed
  every watch-check on a night sail — so he asked the forty-year captain what
  *his* boat-sense was telling him, and the plot moved.

## Failure is a road, not a wall

This DM fails you *forward*: a botched stakeout still shakes loose a lead, a
disastrous performance earns a "come back when you've got better songs." When
you fail, look for the smaller door the narration just opened, and take it.

## Ask the world, not the menu

Phrase questions in-fiction and aim them at people: "whose door does a man
knock on?" beats "list quests." Rumors are real — the DM seeds them from the
world's authored content, and following one (dock gossip → the old fishers →
the captain) lands you in real, written adventures. For genuine table-talk
(rules questions, "can we pause?"), use the out-of-character toggle instead of
breaking character mid-scene.

## People persist. So do promises.

Named NPCs are durable records — the DM remembers who likes you, who's seen
your face twice, what you owe and what you're owed. Debts and favors come due,
so spend them deliberately (our bard banked "a crossing of the bay" and cashed
it three days later to reach a place no coin could hire a boat to). And
reputation travels: botch a song about the local goddess and the room two
districts over may have heard about it.

## Wrap your sessions at chapter breaks

The **Wrap** button ends a session: the DM writes you a spoiler-free recap and
writes itself private continuity notes, which is how the story survives between
sittings. Wrap at natural pauses — a decision made or about to be made, a night
ending, a job done — not mid-scene. A pending decision survives the wrap; the
next session will open right on top of it.

## If a reply ever comes back thin

Rarely, a turn lands as one bare line ("You make your way to the docks.").
Nothing is lost — just ask "What do I see?" or "How did they take it?" and the
DM back-fills the whole scene. (The engine now catches most of these itself and
asks the DM to finish the thought before you ever see it.)

## Quick reference

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| One paragraph: action + intent + "dialogue" | One-line commands, no punctuation |
| One scene-beat per message | Travel + talk + sleep in one message |
| Quote your speech | Narrate private thoughts you don't want seen |
| Change tactics after a failure | Repeat the same ask harder |
| Route tasks through your best stats | Solo-stealth as a charisma bard |
| Use the OOC toggle for table-talk | Break character mid-scene |
| Wrap at chapter breaks | Wrap mid-conversation |
