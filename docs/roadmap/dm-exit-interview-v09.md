# DM exit interview — v0.9 playtest (2026-07-04, Sonnet 5)

At the end of the joint playtest we interviewed the DM itself (via the dev
codeword) about its working conditions. Three questions, condensed. Caveat:
model self-reports on *motive* are unreliable, but reports on *what
information it had or lacked* are well-grounded — and these matched our
observed findings closely.

## 1. What player input made its job easier/harder

Easier (matches `docs/player-guide.md`, independently derived):
- **Stated intent beside the action** — it picks the right skill and pitches
  consequences at the right target without guessing.
- **Dialogue that contains a checkable ask** ("give me records and I'll give
  you a case…") — success criteria arrive pre-written.
- **Callbacks that USE established fiction** (invoking Bromley's owed
  crossing) — the world accumulates instead of resetting.
- **One decision point per message**, however rich the prose.

Harder: intent implied only through *tone* ("I ask true") — it must infer
whether that demands or invites, which changes the NPC's read. Also: fuzzy
check *scope* (one evocative sentence covering what could be one roll or
three).

## 2. Context friction (v2.0 seeds)

- **STORY SO FAR works but doesn't scale**: it's a wall of prose; ten sessions
  in, facts will silently drop. Wants a structured layer besides the prose.
- **No first-class tracker for dangling NPC expectations** (a promise made but
  not yet a quest) — "I have to remember to remember."
- **Deliberate ambiguity needs a flag**: no way to distinguish "unresolved on
  purpose, revisit later" from "forgot to decide" when reading notes cold.
- **Its #1 ask**: tagged short entries in the notebook that persist and surface
  every turn until explicitly closed — `[open promise] …`,
  `[deliberate ambiguity] …` — vs today's write-only prose notes.

## 3. Authored-content templates (use these patterns for future packs)

What ran best — the template:
- **Behavioral lore beats descriptive lore.** Seraphel's entry gives a *rule
  to adjudicate by* ("those who go looking seldom find her… she sinks from
  sight and declines to rise"), not a fact sheet. Facts it can invent; behavior
  under pressure is what it must not guess at.
- **Closed story with an open seam.** The Sirens entry resolves cleanly but
  ends on "the kind of thing that can wash back in on a later tide" — an
  explicit hook that isn't a quest.
- **The quest hook/briefing/outcome-label structure** — "the single best
  mechanical fit to how I actually run tables."

What fought it:
- NPCs that are **physical description with no lever** (no politics, no price,
  no relationship) — it invents those live and then must carry them alone.
- Places with sensory detail but **no standing question** — scenery it has to
  retrofit stakes onto.

Its closing advice, verbatim spirit: *author NPCs and places with a
disposition/rule-of-behavior and a live seam (a debt, a secret, a "someday
this comes back"), and wherever a fact is meant to stay ambiguous, say so
explicitly so I don't burn a turn wondering if there's a briefing I'm
missing.*
